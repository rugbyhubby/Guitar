Section 2 · Step 1
Minor Pentatonic Box 1
The foundation of all lead playing. Master this one shape before anything else.
The Shape — Am at 5th Position

Five notes: A C D E G — Root notes on frets 5 of low E, D string, and high e.

Tap any dot to learn about that note.
Key Points
👆
Index finger anchors fret 5 — it covers all six strings there (like a barre).
🤙
Pinky covers frets 7 & 8 — stretch comfortably, don't tense up.
🔄
Low E and high e are mirrors — same fingering (frets 5 and 8), making the box easier to memorize.
🎯
Three root notes (dark dots) are your anchors — start and end phrases on these.
Practice Goal

Play the scale ascending and descending cleanly at 60 BPM. Every note should ring clearly — no buzzing, no muting adjacent strings. When you can do that, you're ready for Step 2.

Section 2 · Step 2
Core Techniques
Hammer-ons, pull-offs, and slides bring the scale to life. Learn these within Box 1 before expanding.
Hammer-on — h

What it is: Pick the lower note, then "hammer" a finger firmly onto a higher fret on the same string — without picking again. The second note sounds from the impact alone.

In Box 1: Try it on the A string — pick fret 5 (D), then hammer onto fret 7 (E). You should hear both notes clearly from one pick stroke.

Tip: The hammer needs to be firm and placed right behind the fret wire. A weak tap just mutes the string. Aim for the same volume on both notes.
Notation

Written as: 5h7 — the number before h is picked, the number after is hammered.

Common Box 1 hammer-ons: 5h7 on A string, D string, G string. These are your most-used phrases in blues and rock.

Pull-off — p

What it is: The reverse of a hammer-on. Fret both notes first, pick the higher one, then flick your finger off toward the floor — the lower note sounds without re-picking.

In Box 1: Place fingers on fret 5 and fret 7 of the A string. Pick fret 7 (E), then flick your ring/pinky off to sound fret 5 (D).

Tip: The "flick" is the key — don't just lift straight up. A sideways motion toward the floor plucks the string as you pull off, giving the lower note volume.
Combining hammer + pull

Written as: 5h7p5 — pick once, hammer up, pull back down. This is the foundation of legato phrasing and a staple blues lick.

Practice on a single string until both the hammer and pull are equal in volume to the picked note.

Slide — / or \

What it is: Pick a note, then slide your fretting finger up (/) or down (\) to another fret while maintaining pressure — the pitch glides smoothly between the two notes.

In Box 1: Pick fret 5 on the G string (C), then slide up to fret 7 (D). Keep the pressure consistent so the note sustains through the slide.

Tip: You can slide into a note from below without a defined starting fret — called a "scoop." Pick below the target note and slide up to it for an expressive, vocal quality.
Slide vs. Bend

A slide moves along a string to a specific fret. A bend (Step 3) pushes the string sideways to raise the pitch without moving up the neck. Both are essential but feel completely different — slides are smooth and connected, bends are more expressive and vocal.

Section 2 · Step 3
String Bends
The most expressive technique in blues lead. A well-executed bend sounds like the guitar is singing.
How to Bend

Fret a note, pick it, then push the string toward the ceiling (on low strings) or toward the floor (on high strings) while maintaining your pick-hand grip. The pitch rises as you push.

Start here: B string, fret 7 (G). Bend upward a whole step — the target pitch is A. Use your ring finger to bend, with index and middle behind it for support.

Half-step bend

Raises pitch by 1 fret. Subtle and tension-filled. Great for blues expression without full commitment. Written: 7b(½)

Whole-step bend

Raises pitch by 2 frets. The most common blues bend. Classic, expressive, the heart of pentatonic lead. Written: 7b

Best Bend Positions in Box 1
  • G string, fret 5 (C) — bend whole step to D. Strong, mid-range cry.
  • B string, fret 7 (G) — bend whole step to A (the root). Extremely expressive.
  • B string, fret 8 (G#) — half-step bend. Subtle and bluesy.
  • High e, fret 8 (C) — bend whole step to D. Bright, cutting sound at the top.
Targeting pitch accurately

The biggest mistake beginners make is bending without knowing the target pitch. Always know what note you're bending to before you play it. Fret the target note first, listen to it, then come back and bend up to match it. Inaccurate bends are the fastest way to sound out of tune.

Bend + Vibrato: Once you've bent to the target pitch, add vibrato — a small oscillation of the string — by repeatedly bending slightly up and releasing. This is the signature of most great blues lead playing.
Section 2 · Step 4
Phrasing & Improvising
Don't just run the scale — tell a story. Phrasing is about musical sentences, not note lists.
The Golden Rule

A scale is vocabulary. Phrasing is language. Running up and down the pentatonic from bottom to top isn't a phrase — it's a finger exercise. A phrase is 3–6 notes with intention, space, and a destination.

Ingredients of a good phrase
  • Start note — often a chord tone or root
  • Movement — a lick, bend, hammer, or run
  • Landing note — a chord tone to resolve on
  • Space — silence after the phrase
Call and response

Play a phrase (the "call"), then pause. The pause is the "response" — it gives your phrase room to breathe and the listener time to absorb it.

Think of it as a conversation. You say something, then wait. You don't talk over yourself.

Practice Method

1. Record a loop — strum an A minor or A7 chord as a drone. Even 4 bars looped is enough.

2. Improvise for 5 minutes — use only Box 1. Don't worry about sounding good, focus on making phrases rather than running scales.

3. Listen back — identify one phrase that sounded musical. Figure out what made it work (was it the space? The landing note? A bend?). Repeat that intentionally.

Dynamics: Not every note needs to be the same volume. Lighter notes in the middle of a phrase and a stronger hit on the landing note creates natural shape and expression.
Section 2 · Step 5
Boxes 2 & 5 — Connecting the Neck
Box 1 is home. Boxes 2 and 5 let you travel up and down the neck without leaving the key.
The shared notes (thick-bordered dots) at frets 7–8 are your bridges between boxes. Tap any dot.
Box 1 → Box 2 (up the neck)

Box 2 sits at frets 7–10. The shared notes at fret 7–8 are your doorway. When you hit one of those while playing Box 1, you can keep ascending into Box 2.

Shift exercise: Play Box 1 to the top (high e), then keep going up into Box 2. Slow and clean first.
Box 5 (down the neck)

Box 5 sits below Box 1 at frets 2–5. It uses the same shared root notes on fret 5. Once Box 2 feels comfortable, learn Box 5 the same way — connecting downward from Box 1.

Navigation: Think of Box 1 as the center of a map. Box 2 is north (up), Box 5 is south (down).
Section 2 · Step 6
The Major Pentatonic
Same 5-box system, completely different emotional character. The key to blending light and dark in blues.
A Major Pentatonic sits at frets 2–5. Tap any dot to explore each note's role.
A Minor Pentatonic

Notes: A · C · D · E · G

Sound: dark, tense, bluesy. The b3 (C) and b7 (G) are the "blue notes" that give it grit.

A Major Pentatonic

Notes: A · B · C# · E · F#

Sound: bright, resolved, sunny. The major 3rd (C#) and 6th (F#) give it lift and sweetness.

The Blues Trick

The defining move of blues lead is switching between major and minor pentatonic over the same root. Minor builds tension and darkness, major releases it with brightness. BB King, Eric Clapton, and Stevie Ray Vaughan all use this constantly.

Major box sits 3 frets below minor box. When you want brightness, slide down 3 frets. Tension? Slide back up.

They share A (root) and E (5th) — these are your common ground. You can pivot on either note to move between the two scales without it sounding like a mistake.
Section 2 · Step 7
CAGED — E Shape & Major Scale
Map one scale position to a chord shape you already know. This connects rhythm and lead in a single framework.
Dark green = chord tones (root, 3rd, 5th) — your safe landing notes. Outlined = color tones. Tap any dot.
The concept

The A major barre chord (E shape at fret 5) has its notes scattered across the neck. The major scale wraps around that shape — chord tones embedded inside the scale.

When soloing over A major, landing on chord tones always sounds resolved and intentional.

Practice

Step 1: Strum the A major barre chord. Listen to it.

Step 2: Switch to "chord tones only" view and improvise using only those notes. Notice how they all sound "inside."

Step 3: Gradually weave in the color tones as passing notes between chord tones.

Section 2 · Step 8
Improvise Over a Backing Track
This is the final integration step for Section 2. Everything you've learned comes together here.
What to do

Find a blues or rock backing track in the key of A or E on YouTube (search "blues backing track A minor 80bpm"). Play continuously over it for 5+ minutes without stopping.

Don't think about "playing correctly." Think about phrasing, dynamics, and space.

Checklist — things to focus on
  • ✓ Stay in Box 1 first — master one box before wandering
  • ✓ Make phrases, not scale runs — 3-6 notes then silence
  • ✓ Land on root notes (A) at phrase endings
  • ✓ Vary your dynamics — some notes soft, landing notes strong
  • ✓ Use at least one bend per phrase once you're comfortable
  • ✓ Try call-and-response — phrase, pause, phrase, pause
Suggested BPM progression
  • Week 1–2: 60–70 BPM (slow blues)
  • Week 3–4: 80–90 BPM (medium shuffle)
  • Week 5+: 100–120 BPM (upbeat blues)
Ready for Section 3?

When you can improvise confidently over a blues backing track for 5 minutes, using phrases (not runs) with bends and space — you're ready to move fully into Section 3 and combine rhythm + lead.

Section 3 · Step 1
The 12-Bar Blues Form
The foundation of all blues. Four rows, four bars each, three chords. Tap any bar to understand its role.
Key: Variation:
I — tonic (home)
IV — subdominant (lift)
V — dominant (tension)
The 12-bar blues — four rows of four bars. Tap any chord to learn its role.
Reading the grid

Each cell = one bar (4 beats). Read left to right, top to bottom. When you hit bar 12, loop back to bar 1 — it repeats until someone signals a stop.

The three emotions

I (red): Home. Stable, grounded.
IV (blue): Lift. Movement away from home.
V (green): Tension. Strongly wants to resolve back to I.

Section 3 · Step 2
Key of E — Open Chords
E, A, and B7 are open-string friendly and easy to play. The best starting key for the 12-bar blues.
E7
    Practice the full 12-bar in E

    Strum through: E7 → E7 → E7 → E7 → A7 → A7 → E7 → E7 → B7 → A7 → E7 → E7

    Focus on clean chord changes. The goal is to keep time, not to rush between chords.

    Section 3 · Steps 2–4
    Dominant 7th Chord Shapes
    The flat 7th in every chord is what makes blues sound like blues. Learn these five shapes cold.
    A7
      The flat 7th — what makes blues sound like blues

      Every dominant 7th chord contains a flat 7th (b7) — one semitone below the major 7th. In A7 it's the open G string. In E7 it's the open D string. In G7 it's the F on the high e.

      That note creates harmonic tension that wants to resolve — which is exactly the feeling that defines blues music. Without the b7, these are just plain major chords.

      Section 3 · Step 5
      The Shuffle Rhythm
      The shuffle feel is the rhythmic DNA of blues. Without it, you're just playing the chords — not feeling them.
      What is a shuffle?

      Normal eighth notes are evenly spaced: 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 +

      A shuffle swings those eighth notes into a long-short triplet feel:

      DU— DU— DU— DU—

      Think of it as: "trip-let, trip-let, trip-let, trip-let" — but only playing the first and third note of each triplet group.

      Hearing it first

      Before you play it, listen to it. Find any of these songs and listen for the rhythmic pulse:

      • "Sweet Home Chicago" — Robert Johnson / Blues Brothers
      • "Hound Dog" — Elvis Presley
      • "Pride and Joy" — Stevie Ray Vaughan
      • "Johnny B. Goode" — Chuck Berry
      Body trick: Tap your foot on beats 1 and 3 (the "strong" beats). The shuffle rhythm should feel like it's swinging around that foot tap. If your foot is fighting the rhythm, slow down.
      On guitar — the basic pattern

      Root note (down), then 5th (down) — alternating on the low strings. In E: alternate between open E string and fret 2 of A string. In A: fret 0 of A string and fret 2 of D string.

      Adding the 6th

      For the classic blues shuffle, alternate: root, 5th, root, 6th. In E: open E, A string fret 2, open E, A string fret 4. This is the heartbeat of most 12-bar blues songs.

      Section 3 · Step 6
      Quick Change & Turnaround
      These two variations transform the basic 12-bar into something that sounds authentic and musical.
      I
      IV
      V
      Select a variation above to see and hear the difference. Tap any bar for details.
      Quick change

      The IV chord appears in bar 2 (instead of staying on I). Gives the progression more forward momentum and is used in the majority of real blues songs.

      If someone calls "12-bar in A with a quick change" — A7 in bars 1, 3, 4 and D7 in bar 2.

      Turnaround

      Bar 12 switches from I to V (instead of staying on I). The V chord in the last bar launches you straight back to bar 1 with energy and momentum.

      Without a turnaround, the end of the progression sounds like a full stop. With it, the loop feels inevitable and alive.

      Section 3 · Step 7
      Boogie-Woogie Bass Pattern
      Play rhythm and melody at the same time. This is the foundation of solo blues guitar.
      What it is

      Instead of strumming full chords, you play an alternating bass line on the two lowest strings of each chord — root, 5th, root, 6th — while keeping the shuffle feel. This lets one guitarist sound like a full rhythm section.

      Pattern for each chord
      • E chord: Low E open (E) → A string fret 2 (B) → Low E open (E) → A string fret 4 (C#) — repeat
      • A chord: A string open (A) → D string fret 2 (E) → A string open (A) → D string fret 4 (F#) — repeat
      • B chord: A string fret 2 (B) → D string fret 4 (F#) → A string fret 2 (B) → D string fret 6 (G#) — repeat
      Right hand: Use a pick with all downstrokes on each beat, or use your thumb and index in a fingerpicking motion. Downstrokes give more drive; fingerpicking gives more control.
      Play it over the 12-bar

      Apply the appropriate pattern to each chord as you move through the 12-bar form. The chord changes but the pattern shape stays the same — just move it to the right strings.

      Classic examples
      • "Johnny B. Goode" — Chuck Berry
      • "Roll Over Beethoven" — Chuck Berry
      • "Tush" — ZZ Top
      • Most early rock & roll guitar parts
      Section 3 · Step 8
      Minor Blues
      Darker, moodier, more melancholic. The minor blues uses the same 12-bar form with minor 7th chords instead of dominant 7ths.
      Major blues

      Uses dominant 7th chords: A7, D7, E7

      Sound: gritty tension, swing, release. The classic 12-bar feel.

      Minor blues

      Uses minor 7th chords: Am7, Dm7, Em7

      Sound: brooding, melancholic, emotionally heavy. Think slow blues ballads.

      Minor Blues in Am — 12-bar form
      Minor 7th chord shapes
      • Am7: x02010 — Open A string, D fret 2, G open, B fret 1, high e open
      • Dm7: xx0211 — D open, G fret 2, B fret 1, high e fret 1
      • Em7: 020000 — Low E open, A fret 2, all others open
      Lead over minor blues: The A minor pentatonic scale (Box 1 at fret 5) fits perfectly over the minor blues — you don't need to change your scale. The mood difference comes entirely from the chord progression underneath.
      Classic minor blues tracks to study
      • "The Thrill is Gone" — BB King (Bm minor blues)
      • "Little Wing" — Jimi Hendrix (Em)
      • "Since I've Been Loving You" — Led Zeppelin (Cm)
      • "Red House" — Jimi Hendrix (Bb minor blues)
      Section 3 · Step 9
      Rhythm + Lead Together
      This is the complete blues guitarist skill. Rhythm and lead aren't separate — they're a conversation.
      Call and Response — the core idea

      Play 4 bars of rhythm (strumming chords or boogie bass), then switch to lead for 4 bars, answering what the rhythm just said. This mirrors how blues vocalists interact with guitar — the voice calls, the guitar responds.

      Targeting chord tones on changes

      When the chord changes in the 12-bar form, resolve your lead phrase to a chord tone of the new chord on beat 1. This is what separates musical soloing from running scales.

      • Moving to D7 (IV)? Target D, F#, or C at the change
      • Moving to E7 (V)? Target E, G#, or D at the change
      • Back to A7 (I)? Target A, C#, or G at the change
      You don't need to be perfect. Even targeting the root note on every chord change is a massive improvement over ignoring the changes. Add the 3rd and 7th once the root feels natural.
      Practice structure
      • Play full 12-bar rhythm once (chords only)
      • Play full 12-bar lead once (pentatonic only)
      • Alternate: 4 bars rhythm, 4 bars lead
      • Mix freely — switch whenever it feels right
      The goal

      When you can move fluidly between rhythm and lead over the 12-bar form — making musical phrases, hitting chord tones, and keeping the groove — you're playing blues guitar.

      Everything else is refinement.

      Section 3 · Step 10
      Learn a Full Blues Song
      Theory becomes music when you apply it to a real song. This step ties everything together.
      Why this matters

      Learning a complete song forces you to apply rhythm, chord changes, shuffle feel, and lead phrasing in a real musical context. It also gives you something to actually play — which is the whole point.

      Recommended starting songs
      • "Pride and Joy" — Stevie Ray Vaughan (E, shuffle, boogie bass pattern, great intro lick)
      • "Sweet Home Chicago" — Robert Johnson (A, quick change, singalong feel)
      • "Crossroads" — Cream / Robert Johnson (A, fast shuffle, classic rock blues)
      • "Before You Accuse Me" — Eric Clapton (E, medium shuffle, very learnable)
      • "Texas Flood" — SRV (G minor, slow blues, great for minor blues study)
      How to learn a song
      • Listen 5+ times before picking up the guitar
      • Identify the key and the 12-bar structure
      • Learn the rhythm part first (chord shapes + shuffle)
      • Add the intro lick or signature riff
      • Learn the solo last, phrase by phrase
      You've made it

      Completing this roadmap means you understand the pentatonic scales, basic lead techniques, the 12-bar form, dominant 7th chords, shuffle rhythm, and how rhythm and lead connect.

      That's the entire foundation of blues guitar. Everything from here is deepening what you already have.
